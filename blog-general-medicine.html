Hypertension: The Silent Danger

What every adult needs to know about high blood pressure.

Home›Blog›Hypertension
Dr. R. Prithviraj General & Emergency Medicine · May 2025 · 5 min read

In an outpatient clinic, hypertension is the most common diagnosis I make on a person who walked in feeling perfectly fine. There is no fever, no pain, no obvious sign of anything wrong. Just a number on a blood pressure cuff — and a quiet conversation about how that number, left alone, can quietly take years off a life.

Hypertension is called the "silent killer" not because it kills suddenly, but because it does its damage invisibly. You can have dangerously high blood pressure for a decade with no symptoms, while your arteries stiffen, your heart muscle thickens, your kidneys scar, and the small vessels of your eyes and brain are slowly stripped of their elasticity. When the first dramatic symptom finally arrives — a stroke, a heart attack, kidney failure — the harm is already done.

What the Numbers Mean

Blood pressure is recorded as two numbers: the higher (systolic) is the pressure when the heart contracts; the lower (diastolic) is the pressure between beats. For most adults:

  • Normal: below 120/80 mmHg
  • Elevated: 120–129 / below 80
  • Stage 1 hypertension: 130–139 / 80–89
  • Stage 2 hypertension: 140/90 or higher
  • Hypertensive crisis: above 180/120 — seek care immediately

A single high reading does not make a diagnosis. We confirm with multiple readings over several visits, and increasingly with a 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitor that tracks pressure during sleep, work, and stress.

Why Are So Many of Us at Risk?

Urban Indian lifestyles have created near-perfect conditions for hypertension. Long sedentary work hours, high-salt processed food, ongoing stress, irregular sleep, and rising rates of obesity all push blood pressure upward. Genetics matter too: if a parent or sibling has hypertension, your risk is two to three times higher. The result is that we now see hypertension routinely in people in their 30s, not just their 60s.

The damage from uncontrolled hypertension is silent for years — then loud in a single, catastrophic moment.

What High BP Does to Your Body

  • Heart: thickens the heart muscle (left ventricular hypertrophy), leading to heart failure and atrial fibrillation
  • Brain: the single biggest risk factor for stroke — both clot strokes and bleeds
  • Kidneys: small kidney vessels scar over years, ending in chronic kidney disease and sometimes dialysis
  • Eyes: retinal damage that can permanently impair vision
  • Arteries: accelerates atherosclerosis throughout the body, raising heart attack risk

Home Monitoring — A Game Changer

Modern guidelines emphasise out-of-clinic blood pressure measurement. A reliable home BP machine (upper-arm cuff, validated — not wrist devices) used twice a day for a week gives a much more accurate picture than a single rushed reading in the clinic. For patients with suspected white-coat hypertension or wide fluctuations, we offer a 24-hour ambulatory BP monitor in-house.

Treatment: Lifestyle First, Medication When Needed

For stage 1 hypertension without other risk factors, we start with lifestyle: a DASH-style diet (more vegetables, fruits, whole grains; less salt and red meat), regular aerobic exercise (30 minutes most days), weight loss if needed, limiting alcohol, and managing stress and sleep. These changes alone can lower systolic pressure by 10–15 mmHg — equivalent to one antihypertensive medication.

For stage 2, or stage 1 with diabetes, kidney disease, or established cardiovascular disease, medications are added early. Modern antihypertensives are well-tolerated, taken once daily, and proven to dramatically reduce stroke and heart attack risk over a lifetime.

Get Checked, Even If You Feel Fine

This is the most important sentence of the article: do not wait for symptoms. Hypertension is the easiest cardiovascular risk factor to detect — it takes 90 seconds with a cuff — and one of the most treatable. Every adult should know their numbers by age 30, and check yearly thereafter.

If you have not had your blood pressure checked in the past year, book a routine appointment. A small action today can change the trajectory of the next thirty years.
