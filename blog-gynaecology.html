PCOS Decoded

What every woman should know about Polycystic Ovary Syndrome.

Home›Blog›PCOS
Dr. S.V. Priyanka Gynaecologist & Obstetrician · April 2025 · 5 min read

If I had to name the single most under-recognised health condition affecting Indian women in their 20s and 30s, it would be PCOS — Polycystic Ovary Syndrome. Despite affecting roughly one in five Indian women, it is still dismissed in many homes as "just irregular periods" or "you'll be fine once you get married." It is much more than that, and dismissing it has long-term consequences.

PCOS is a hormonal and metabolic disorder. The ovaries produce too much androgen (male-pattern hormone), ovulation becomes irregular, and the body's response to insulin starts to weaken. Each of these three threads — hormones, ovulation, and insulin — explains a different cluster of symptoms.

The Symptoms to Recognise

PCOS often begins in the teens and twenties, but goes unnamed for years. The classic features include:

  • Irregular periods — cycles longer than 35 days, or fewer than 8 periods a year
  • Excess hair growth on the face, chin, chest, or abdomen (hirsutism)
  • Persistent acne on the face, chest, or back, often resistant to standard treatment
  • Hair thinning on the scalp
  • Weight gain, especially around the abdomen, that is difficult to lose
  • Difficulty conceiving due to irregular ovulation
  • Darkened skin patches on the neck, armpits, or groin (acanthosis nigricans) — a sign of insulin resistance

Not every woman with PCOS has all these symptoms. Some have only irregular periods; others present with infertility as the first sign.

Why It Matters Beyond the Cosmetic

The acne and hair growth may be what brings women in to see me. The long-term picture is what makes PCOS a condition we must take seriously:

  • Type 2 diabetes — women with PCOS are 4 times more likely to develop diabetes
  • Cardiovascular disease — higher rates of hypertension, abnormal cholesterol, and heart disease
  • Endometrial cancer — long gaps without periods cause the uterine lining to thicken, raising cancer risk over decades
  • Infertility and pregnancy complications — gestational diabetes, hypertension, and miscarriage are more common
  • Mental health — depression and anxiety are significantly more common in women with PCOS, partly due to hormonal effects, partly to the burden of symptoms

PCOS is manageable — but only if it's named, understood, and addressed early. Don't accept "irregular periods" as just a quirk of your body.

How We Diagnose PCOS

Diagnosis is based on the Rotterdam criteria: at least two of three features must be present — irregular ovulation, signs of high androgens (clinical or on blood test), and polycystic-appearing ovaries on ultrasound. Blood tests usually include LH/FSH ratio, free testosterone, DHEAS, AMH, prolactin, TSH, and a fasting blood sugar with insulin to check for insulin resistance. A pelvic ultrasound rounds out the picture.

Treatment: Personalised, Layered, Effective

Lifestyle is the foundation. Even a modest 5–10% weight reduction can restore regular periods, improve fertility, and reduce diabetes risk. A diet that minimises refined carbohydrates and added sugars, combined with regular strength and cardio exercise, addresses the underlying insulin resistance.

Medications are layered on as needed. Metformin improves insulin sensitivity. Combined oral contraceptive pills regulate cycles, reduce androgens, and protect the uterine lining. For women trying to conceive, ovulation induction with letrozole or clomiphene is highly effective. Persistent hirsutism may need spironolactone or laser treatment.

Myths to Let Go Of

  • "PCOS means I can't have children." Not true — most women with PCOS conceive, often with simple ovulation induction.
  • "It will go away after marriage." No. PCOS is a lifelong condition that needs lifelong attention.
  • "Only overweight women get PCOS." Lean PCOS exists. Up to 30% of women with PCOS have a normal BMI.
  • "It's nothing serious." It's a leading cause of infertility and a significant risk factor for diabetes and heart disease.

The Path Forward

If your periods are irregular, if acne and hair growth feel out of proportion, or if you are struggling to conceive — please get evaluated. PCOS is one of the most studied, most treatable conditions in modern gynaecology. The earlier we begin care, the easier it is to prevent the long-term complications.
