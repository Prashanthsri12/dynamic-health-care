Sleep Health

Why Sleep Is Medicine

Understanding sleep disorders and how we treat them.

Home›Blog›Sleep
Dr. K. Sankara Subramanian Consultant Neurologist · June 2025 · 5 min read

For decades, sleep was considered a passive state — time the body simply spent recovering from the demands of the day. Modern neuroscience tells a very different story. During sleep, your brain consolidates memories, clears metabolic waste, regulates hormones that govern hunger and stress, and repairs the immune system. Sleep is not a luxury. It is medicine, prescribed nightly by your own biology.

And yet, many of my patients arrive in clinic having not had a truly restorative night's sleep for years. They've adapted to a foggy morning, a 3 PM slump, and a constant low-grade tiredness as if these were inevitable. Often they are not. They are symptoms.

What Healthy Sleep Looks Like

A normal night of sleep cycles through two main stages every 90 minutes or so: NREM (non-rapid eye movement, deep restorative sleep) and REM (rapid eye movement, when most dreaming occurs and memory is processed). Healthy adults need 7–9 hours of sleep, and crucially they need to complete these cycles uninterrupted. Wake repeatedly through the night — even briefly, even without remembering it — and the architecture of sleep crumbles.

The Common Sleep Disorders

Four conditions account for the vast majority of sleep complaints we see:

  • Insomnia — trouble falling or staying asleep, often driven by stress, anxiety, or poor sleep hygiene.
  • Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA) — the airway repeatedly collapses during sleep, causing dozens or even hundreds of brief awakenings. The hallmark sign is loud, irregular snoring, often with pauses witnessed by a bed partner.
  • Restless Leg Syndrome — an uncomfortable urge to move the legs, especially at night, that disrupts sleep onset.
  • Narcolepsy — excessive daytime sleepiness with sudden sleep attacks, often misdiagnosed for years.

If you snore loudly, wake up unrefreshed, or catch yourself nodding off during the day — these are not personality traits. They are clues.

Warning Signs You Shouldn't Ignore

Talk to a neurologist if you notice any of these patterns:

  • Loud snoring with pauses in breathing observed by your partner
  • Waking up gasping or choking
  • Persistent daytime sleepiness despite 7+ hours in bed
  • Morning headaches and poor concentration
  • Difficulty falling asleep most nights for more than a month
  • Acting out dreams — punching, kicking, talking loudly while asleep

How a Sleep Study Works

A sleep study, formally called polysomnography, is the gold standard for diagnosing sleep disorders. The patient sleeps overnight while sensors record brain waves (EEG), eye movement, muscle tone, heart rhythm, airflow, breathing effort, and blood oxygen levels. The next morning we can tell exactly how many times the airway collapsed, how deeply you reached the restorative stages of sleep, and whether your oxygen dropped to dangerous levels.

At Dynamic Health Care, sleep studies are performed in-house and reviewed by our neurology team. There's no need to be admitted to a hospital for it.

Treatment Is Effective — And Often Transformative

For OSA, the first-line treatment is a CPAP or BiPAP machine, which gently pressurises the airway during sleep to keep it open. Many patients describe their first week on CPAP as "the best sleep of my life." For insomnia, we treat the underlying cause — whether it's anxiety, depression, irregular schedules, or learned associations — with cognitive behavioural therapy and, where appropriate, short-term medication. For restless legs, addressing iron deficiency and using specific medications brings rapid relief.

Sleep Hygiene Basics

Whatever your situation, these habits help everyone:

  • Keep a fixed wake-up time, even on weekends
  • Get bright light exposure within an hour of waking
  • Avoid caffeine after 2 PM
  • No screens for the last 60 minutes before bed
  • Keep the bedroom cool, dark, and used only for sleep

If you've been quietly tolerating poor sleep, you don't have to. A good neurological evaluation often turns up something we can fix — sometimes dramatically. Sleep, after all, is medicine.
